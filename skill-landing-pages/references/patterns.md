# Landing Page Patterns Reference

Distilled from proven conversion patterns. Each ingredient has named patterns with when-to-use guidance. Use this to select specific patterns when reviewing, planning, or building.

---

## 1. LOGOS

Logos establish baseline credibility. Keep them small. They should register, not dominate.

Patterns:
- **Word Mark**: Just the company name in a good typeface. Safest option if you're not a designer. Keep it simple, avoid trends.
- **Symbol**: Abstract icon. Requires design skill. Skip if you don't have it.
- **Combination**: Word mark + symbol. Hard to pull off without design chops. Simple combinations (letter in a shape) work.
- **Tagline**: Add a slogan, product category, product line, or version near the logo. Use to clarify what the company does or to signal newness.

Rule: If the logo is drawing more attention than the headline, it's too big.

---

## 2. NAVIGATION

Navigation must serve the conversion goal. Every link that leads away is a risk.

Patterns:
- **No Navigation**: Best for single-product PPC landing pages. Forces visitors to scroll and read. Disadvantage on very long pages.
- **Within-Page Navigation**: Scrolling nav bar with anchor links to page sections. Good for long pages. Keeps visitors on-page.
- **Navigation Links**: Links to other pages. Two types: funnel-advancing (features, pricing) and tangential (blog, about). Tangential links are the risk. Only include if you have a loop-closing strategy.
- **Navigation CTA**: A call-to-action button in the nav bar. Accent color, stands out from other links. Almost always a good idea.
- **Login Button**: For SaaS with existing users. Make it visually subordinate to the main CTA. Smaller, less colorful.
- **Other Nav Elements**: Language selector, phone number (credibility + mobile UX), social proof in nav (e.g. "100k+ customers"), announcements, urgency timers.

Rule: If you include navigation links, you must have a way to bring visitors back.

---

## 3. HEADLINES

The headline is the most important element on the page. Five times more people read the headline than the body copy.

Patterns:
- **Benefit**: States the main benefit directly. Strong verb. "Now you can..." Best for audiences not yet sold on the concept. E.g. "Organize your team's work."
- **Easy Way**: "The [easy/fastest/smartest] way to [X]." Effective when your product simplifies something seen as complex. Useless if the thing is already simple.
- **Benefit & Audience**: Names the target audience explicitly. "Project management for freelancers." Requires audience to self-identify with the label.
- **Pain Remover**: States the problem, then implies removal. "With my product, you can X without Y." Resonates when visitors are searching for solutions to a specific pain.
- **Product Description**: Simply describes what the product is. "A monthly box of dog goodies." Works when the audience already knows they want this category of product (high awareness).
- **Double/Triple Benefit**: Two or three benefits in one headline. Can use rhyming. Don't go beyond 3—dilutes the message.
- **Question**: Asks the visitor a question. "Problem" questions ("Tired of X?") or "Challenge" questions ("Think you know Y?"). Prompts immediate engagement.
- **Problem/Question**: States the problem bluntly, then asks what they'll do about it.
- **Product Category**: Describes the category + 1-2 adjectives for positioning. "Simple, beautiful help desk software." Works for medium-high awareness audiences shopping the category.
- **Social Proof**: Uses an impressive statistic. "89 of the top 100 school districts use Clever." Requires genuinely impressive numbers.
- **Testimonial**: Extracts a headline from a customer quote. More authentic voice. Works when customers articulate the value better than you.
- **Comparison**: "Do X like [famous person/company]." Audience must know the reference.
- **Promise**: Makes a specific, quantified promise. "You'll have X in Y days." Precision increases believability.
- **Category & Audience**: "[Product] is [category] for [audience]." Template: "Xero is accounting for small business."
- **Number One**: Only use if you can prove it. Without proof, it's hype. Awards, benchmarks, or rankings required.

Rules:
- Make it relevant to the visitor's current mental conversation.
- Make it about the customer, not the company. Never start with "We."
- Clear beats clever. Don't confuse headlines with slogans.
- No nonsense, generalities, or marketese.
- Make it big. It deserves the most visual weight.

Matching headlines to awareness levels:
- **Unaware**: Pain remover, problem/question, question
- **Problem-aware**: Benefit, easy way, pain remover
- **Solution-aware**: Product description, product category, category & audience
- **Product-aware**: Comparison, double benefit, social proof, number one
- **Most aware**: Promise, testimonial, social proof

## 4. SUBHEADS

Subheads support the headline. They are NOT secondary headlines—they expand, clarify, or add specificity.

Patterns:
- **Benefits**: Expands the headline with more detail. "So you can..."
- **Bullet List**: 2-3 top benefits summarized. Quick scan.
- **Product Description**: "[Product] is..." One or two sentences. Use "you/your" not "we."
- **Explanation**: Explains how the benefit is delivered. Can include risk removal ("No coding required").
- **Social Proof**: Stats, user counts, authority claims in the subhead.
- **Call to Action**: Pairs a CTA with the subhead. Works for very attractive offers (free trial, limited offer).

---

## 5. HERO IMAGES

The main visual. Must make a strong first impression.

Patterns:
- **Problem Image**: Illustrates the problem the product solves. Creates empathy. Good for crisis/pain-driven products.
- **Result Image**: Shows the outcome of using the product. Before/after variant.
- **Product Image**: The physical product itself. High quality mandatory. Use for physical goods.
- **User Image**: Person interacting with the product. Humanizes. Can be generic (person + laptop).
- **Interactive Demo**: Let visitors try the product on the page. High effort, high payoff for demonstrable products.
- **Product Screenshot**: Software equivalent of product photo. Trade-off: visibility vs. space.
- **Product in Use/Context**: Product in its natural environment. More realistic than isolated product shots.
- **Device Image**: Product shown on devices. Justified for mobile-first products. Cliche risk otherwise.
- **Before/After**: Visual contrast. Powerful when the transformation is dramatic.
- **Abstract Background**: Gradients, patterns, polygons. No product shown. Shifts emphasis to headline/CTA. Use when product visuals aren't compelling.
- **App Icon**: For apps discovered in app stores. Shows visitors they're in the right place. Doesn't demo benefits.
- **Video**: High engagement if done right. Always provide text alternative. Never autoplay with sound. Make play button unmissable.

Rules:
- No carousels. Ever.
- No low-quality images. Compression artifacts scream amateur.
- No cliched stock photos (people laughing at salads, handshakes in conference rooms).
- Image size vs. page speed: optimize, don't overload.

---

## 6. CALLS TO ACTION

The action you want visitors to take. THE most important conversion element.

Patterns:
- **Simple CTA**: Button with text. Make it look like a button. High-contrast color. Verb-first text.
- **Expanded CTA**: Button + reassurance line. "No credit card required." "Easy setup, no coding." "Free for 14 days." Removes last-second doubt.
- **Secondary CTA**: For visitors not ready to commit. "Learn more" or "Watch video (2 min)." Less visual weight than primary CTA.
- **Opt-In CTA**: Email field + button on the page itself. Captures leads even if visitors don't complete signup.
- **Benefit CTA**: CTA placed within benefit sections. Benefit + testimonial + CTA = powerful combo.
- **Bottom CTA**: Mandatory at page bottom. Pair with headline and social proof. Nobody should have to scroll up to convert.
- **Footer CTA**: Last-chance subtle CTA in the footer.
- **Audience Segmentation CTA**: Two CTAs for two distinct audiences (e.g. buyers vs. sellers on a marketplace).

Rules:
- One conversion goal per page. Repeat the same CTA, don't diversify.
- Start CTA text with a verb describing what the visitor gets. Use "I want to..." test.
- Never use "Submit."
- Make it impossible to miss. Big, contrasting, repeated at every natural decision point.
- Match CTA aggressiveness to awareness level. Unaware visitors need "Learn more," not "Buy now."

## 7. BENEFITS

Why the visitor should care. Answers "What's in it for me?"

Patterns:
- **Simple Benefit Section**: Headline + short text + image. Side-by-side or stacked layout.
- **Multiple Benefit Section**: 2-3 smaller benefits in a row. For secondary benefits.
- **Complex Benefit Section**: Interactive, tabbed, or hierarchical. Requires engaged visitors.
- **Interactive Benefit Section**: Calculators, sliders, product demos within benefits. Lets visitors "touch" the value.
- **Comparison Benefit Section**: Shows your product is better than alternatives. Before/after, old way/new way.
- **Reason Why Section**: Numbered list of reasons to buy. Leverages list curiosity.
- **Objection Countering Section**: Anticipates and defuses specific doubts. Be careful not to introduce new objections.

Rules:
- Apply the "So what?" test. If you can ask "So what?" it's a feature, not a benefit.
- Answer with "So you can..." to find the real benefit.
- Keep benefit text short: 4 lines max. Nobody reads paragraphs.
- Good headlines on benefits are just as important as the main headline.
- Pair benefits with relevant proof (testimonial about that specific benefit).

---

## 8. FEATURES

What the customer is buying. Factual description of what's included.

Patterns:
- **List of Features**: Simple list. Can include links to demos.
- **Grid of Features**: Icon + title + short text per feature. Common and effective.
- **Hierarchical List**: Grouped/nested features for complex products.
- **Product Breakdown**: Exploded view or deep dive. Apple-style. High production value.
- **Coming Soon Features**: Hints at upcoming additions. Can convince fence-sitters.

Rule: Features matter more for high-awareness audiences. Low-awareness audiences need benefits first.

---

## 9. HOW IT WORKS

Explains process. Two purposes: (1) show simplicity, (2) prove the mechanism behind claims.

Patterns:
- **Classic 1-2-3(-4)**: 3-4 columns, each step. Keep to 3-4 max if showing simplicity.
- **In-Depth How It Works**: Longer, more detailed walkthrough. Works when simplicity IS the main benefit.
- **Video How It Works**: For complex products hard to explain in text. Always pair with text alternative.

---

## 10. USE CASES

Segment your audience and show relevance to each segment.

Patterns:
- **Vertical Segmentation**: By industry.
- **Business Size Segmentation**: SMB vs enterprise.
- **Function Segmentation**: By role (PM, engineer, support).
- **End-Goal Segmentation**: By what the user wants to accomplish.

---

## 11. PROOF

Supports claims. Place adjacent to the claims, not in a separate ghetto.

Patterns:
- **Testimonials**: Quote + real name + photo minimum. Add company, role for B2B. Video if possible. Extract headline from quote. Embedded tweets for authenticity.
- **Client Logos**: Introduce with a line ("Trusted by..."). Feature most impressive logos. Show count if large.
- **Press Mentions**: Logos + quotes from articles. Only if the publications are recognizable to your audience.
- **Reviews**: Rating + source. Quantitative impression.
- **Number of Users**: "Join 100,000+ teams." Only works if the number is impressive.
- **Usage Statistics**: Metrics that show scale (API calls, revenue managed, hours tracked).
- **Founder Authority**: Leverage founder's reputation if they're known in the domain.
- **Product Results**: Precise metrics from real customers. "89% improvement." Precision > round numbers.
- **Investor Logos**: For startups. Leverage VC prestige.
- **Scientific Authority**: Academic research, methodology backing. Niche but powerful.
- **Awards**: Only if relevant and prestigious.
- **Case Studies**: Testimonial on steroids. Mandatory for complex B2B.
- **Mini Case Studies**: Testimonial + business result + short description. Faster to produce than full case studies.

Rules:
- Pair proof with specific claims, not in a separate section.
- Start proof early (hero section if possible).
- Specificity > generality. "Reduced churn by 23%" beats "great product."
- Precise numbers beat round numbers for believability.

---

## 12. FAQs

FAQs are a SALES tool, not customer support. They counter objections.

Patterns:
- **Standard FAQ**: List of Q&A. Content matters more than layout.
- **Collapsible FAQ**: For large question sets. Visitors scan headings.
- **"Yes" List FAQ**: Every question phrased so the answer is "Yes!" Basecamp-style.

Rules:
- Every question must either counter an objection or add value.
- Never introduce objections the visitor hadn't considered.
- Never phrase questions negatively ("What if I'm not happy?"). Reframe: "Do you offer a guarantee?"
- Good patterns: "Will it work for [me/my situation]?" "Do I need [hidden cost]?" "How is it different from [competitor]?" "Is it easy to [start/use/cancel]?"

---

## 13. PRICING TABLES

Patterns:
- **Headline**: Not "Pricing." Use a benefit or CTA. "Go get the music."
- **Number of Plans**: 3 is standard. Beyond 4 is danger zone. Single price is simplest.
- **Plan Names**: Clear labels > creative names. "Starter/Growth/Enterprise" > "Kappa/Ronin/Fugu."
- Highlight the recommended plan. Make comparison easy. Show where plans differ, not where they're identical. Always include CTA.

---

## 14. FORMS

Patterns: keep it minimal.
- Minimize fields. Only ask what you absolutely need right now.
- Top-aligned labels. Not inline (they disappear when typing).
- Mark optional fields explicitly.
- High-visibility design: contrasting background.
- Give the form a headline explaining what visitors get.
- Input validation.
- Verb-based CTA button. "Get My Free Guide" not "Submit."

---

## 15. FOOTERS

Patterns:
- **Sitemap**: Standard links. Good UX, debatable for conversion.
- **CTA**: Repeat the main CTA. Always a good idea.
- **Newsletter Opt-in**: Capture emails for future marketing.
- **Blog Links**: Show expertise. Engagement play.
- **Contact Info**: Phone, email, address. Credibility signal.
- **Social Media**: Links to accounts. Minor social proof. Keep de-emphasized.
- **Internationalization**: Language selector if not in main nav.
- **Legal**: Terms, privacy, copyright. Required but won't close sales.

Priority order for footer elements: CTA > newsletter > content > sitemap > legal.

---

## HERO SECTION RECIPES

Five recipes, each building on the essentials:

1. **Simple**: Headline + Subhead + Hero Image + CTA. Bare minimum. Works for simple/free products.
2. **Proof**: Simple + proof elements (testimonials, logos, press). For products needing credibility boost.
3. **Features**: Simple + quick feature list/grid. For high-awareness audiences who know the category.
4. **Benefits**: Simple + 2-3 benefit summaries. For products needing to sell the concept.
5. **Benefits + Proof**: Simple + benefits + proof. Most complex. Risk: visual overload. Works if executed cleanly.

Rule: Don't overload the hero section. Visitors can only absorb so much in the first few seconds. Push depth below the fold.
